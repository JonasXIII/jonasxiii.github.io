How This AI Thinks

A plain-language tour of the ideas behind every toggle in the playground - and things to try that let you see each one for yourself.

The basic idea: looking ahead

An Abalone AI can't "see" a good move the way a person recognizes a face - it has to search. At its core, it plays out an imaginary sequence of moves: "if I move here, my opponent will probably respond there, then I'd respond here…" and so on, a few moves deep. This is called minimax search - the AI assumes both sides play their best move, and picks the first move that leads to the best outcome once all that future play is accounted for.

The lookahead depth slider controls how many moves ("plies") ahead it searches. Depth 1 just asks "what's the best immediate move?" Depth 4 asks "what's the best move, assuming my opponent then plays their best reply, and I play my best reply to that, three more times?" Deeper search sees further into the consequences of a move - but the number of possible move sequences explodes exponentially with depth, so it also gets slower fast.

Why an evaluation function is needed at all

A full game of Abalone can run for dozens of moves. No computer can search all the way to the end of the game in reasonable time - the tree of possibilities is astronomically large. So the search has to stop a few moves ahead and guess how good the resulting position is. That guess is the evaluation function - a formula that looks at a board and returns a number: positive if it favors the AI, negative if it favors the opponent.

In this playground, the evaluation is a weighted sum of six independent ideas ("heuristics") you can turn on/off and reweight in AI Settings:

  • Marble differential - how many marbles each side has left. The most direct signal, since losing 6 marbles is literally how you lose.
  • Center control - marbles near the middle of the board are harder to push off (they have more neighbors backing them up) and threaten more directions at once.
  • Cohesion - marbles that stay grouped with same-color neighbors are safer; isolated marbles are easy 2-on-1 or 3-on-1 targets.
  • Edge safety - marbles sitting on the outer ring are one push away from falling off the board entirely.
  • Triple formations - a line of 3 same-color marbles is the strongest possible attacking shape (it can push 1 or 2 opposing marbles).
  • Mobility - how many legal moves a side has. A very accurate signal of flexibility, but the most expensive one to compute - toggling it off noticeably speeds up deep searches, which is why even the strongest built-in bot leaves it off.

Try this: turn every heuristic off except marble differential, then watch your AI play. It'll only care about immediate captures and ignore positioning entirely - a good way to see why the other five heuristics matter.

Alpha-beta pruning: same answer, far less work

Searching every possible sequence of moves ("plain minimax") gets slow fast. Alpha-beta pruning is a way to skip over branches of the search tree that can't possibly change the outcome - if the AI already knows a rival move guarantees a better result, it doesn't need to keep examining a line that's already looking worse than that, once it's found one reply bad enough to prove it. Crucially, pruning never changes which move the AI picks - it just gets there faster by not wasting time on moves it can already tell aren't worth exploring.

A real measurement from this project: searching the opening position to depth 2 with default settings examined 1,981 positions with pruning off, and only 400 positions with pruning on - a 5x reduction - while landing on the exact same best move and the exact same score. That's the whole trade: pruning buys speed, not accuracy.

Try this: turn Alpha-beta pruning off in AI Settings. Notice the lookahead slider gets capped at 2 - without pruning, depth 3+ would take too long to stay responsive. That cap is a direct, visible consequence of how much slower unpruned search is.

Caching: never solve the same position twice

Different move sequences can arrive at the exact same board position (e.g. move A then B can land on the same board as move B then A). Re-searching a position the AI has already fully analyzed is wasted work. A transposition table remembers "I already searched this exact position to this depth, and here's what I found" - so if it comes up again, the AI reuses the answer instead of recomputing it.

The "Cache hits" and "Cache size" numbers in the Thinking panel show this happening live. In Batch Run mode, later games in a batch often think noticeably faster than the first one - the cache built up during earlier games is still warm and gets reused.

Opening principles

The first couple of moves of a game are well understood in Abalone: pull your back-row marbles toward the center and avoid the edges, rather than searching from scratch. When "Use opening principles for first moves" is on, the AI skips the (comparatively slow) full search for its first two moves per side and instead picks whichever legal move most improves center control and edge safety - faster, and it reflects real opening theory rather than a fixed memorized sequence.

Why the weaker bots feel less "robotic"

A bot that always plays the mathematically-best move at a shallow depth can feel oddly predictable and easy to exploit once you spot the pattern. The two weakest bots (Novice and Apprentice) add a chance of ignoring their own search result and making a random legal move instead - a small dose of randomness that makes their play harder to predict and more forgiving to play against, at the cost of occasionally blundering. The stronger bots drop this to zero as they lean more on genuinely stronger search instead.

Try it yourself: Batch Run mode

Watching one game tells you what happened once. Batch Run mode plays many games back-to-back with no animation and reports the aggregate result, which is a much better way to answer questions like:

  • Does turning alpha-beta off actually make the AI weaker, or just slower? (Configure two identical AIs except for that one toggle, run 10-20 games, compare win rates.)
  • How much does lookahead depth alone matter? Pit a depth-1 AI against a depth-4 AI with the same heuristics.
  • Is the mobility heuristic worth its cost? Compare think-time and win rate with it on vs off.
  • How strong is each built-in bot, really? Run your default-configured AI against each level and see where the win rate crosses 50%.

Every batch result is saved locally so you can build up a small experiment log over time.
